Overview¶

This Notebook is purposed for the documentation of my research conducted under the Supervision of Dr. Madison Smith (Applied Physics Lab). This project aims to investigate wind patterns in arctic regions as a method of determining feasibilities of prospective experiments in Dr. Smith's upcoming Arctic expedition. Published research suggests a correlation between sustained wind speeds below 6 m/s and increased temperature/decreased salinity in arctic open-water ice leads (Holland, 2003). The combination of higher temperature and lower salinity would lead to a higher differential of lateral ice melting while basal and surface melting would hypothetically remain constant. This change in lateral melt is unaccounted for in current climate models. Datasets included in this project are manipulated with xarray, a python package purposed for the creation and manipulation of large datasets.

Reading in Wind Data¶

The primary step required is to read in all wind reanalysis data. The Datasets used are NOAA NCEP/NCAR Reanalysis 1 datasets (available for download here), which combine observational and simulation data to provide a comprehensive overview of the Wind speeds. The datasets are in vectorized format and are organized by year. To begin, we choose to investigate the last 10 years. Due to the nature and layouts of the datasets, it is desirable to combine the vectorized forms of the wind data into a single dataset comprising of all years in the decade-long range.

Creating a Mask for the Data¶

Given that we are investigating the wind reanalysis data for purposes related to sea ice, it is necessary to mask data over land as wind speeds interact differently with land than they do with open ocean/sea ice. To do this, we will use the high resolution land/sea mask provided with NCEP/NCAR data, which has a resolution of 720 latitude coordinates, and 1440 longitude coordinates.

This mask is rotated from the actual values by 180 degrees longitude (see below)

Adjusting the Mask¶

See how Greenland is off by 180 degrees? We can fix this by using the roll command in numpy, which rotates the values about a selected axis.

Reindexing the Dataset & adding the mask¶

Now that we have the land mask in its proper orientation, we can add the masking coordinates to the dataset. Since we used the high resolution data for the mask (since a lower resolution mask would inevitably produce the undesired result of masking land and sea), it is necessary to reindex our data accordingly, so that the latitudes and longitudes of the mask and data match up. The key here is that we are not interpolating our data - such a process would result in some "sea" grid cells becoming an everage of "land" cells and other "sea" cells. Insteas, we reindex the data, using a "nearest value" protocol, which breaks each initial cell into roughly 57 smaller cells, but each of the smaller cells has the exact value of the initial cell.

Finally, we add the mask to the data as a coordinate, so that it can easily be used as a parameter upon which the other variables rely.

In [5]:
#Getting the Mask
ls_mask=xr.open_dataset('LandSeaMasks/lsmask3.nc')

#eliminate time coordinate from mask dataset
ls_mask=ls_mask.squeeze()

#Pull latitude and Longitude dimensions from mask data
lat=ls_mask.lat
lon=ls_mask.lon

#rotating the mask
rotated=np.roll(ls_mask.lsmask.values,720, axis=1)

#Creating xr Data Array for the mask
mask=xr.DataArray(rotated, coords=[lat,lon], dims=['lat','lon'])

#reindexing the Wind dataset
FullWind=FullWind.reindex_like(mask, 'nearest')

#Adding the mask to the dataset
FullWind.coords['mask']=(('lat','lon'), mask)

#Close mask variables
mask.close()
ls_mask.close()

#delete unnecessary variables
del lat, lon, rotated, ls_mask

Isolating Arctic data & adding Wind Magnitude Variable¶

Now that we have loaded and combined all of the datasets in our desired range, it is essential to isolate both summer Months and arctic regions. To isolate Summer data, we can restrict the range to the months including May, June, July, August and September. The Dataset labeled "FullWind" can be preserved, but for the effeciency of the code, we delete this dataset. Comment out the final line in the below cell to keep this dataset. The arctic regions are isolated by using latitudes above 65 degrees (the 619th value in the latitude dataset corresponds to the highest latitude below 65 degrees), and are saved in the dataset so called "FullWind_arctic". Finally, we add a variable for the Wind magnitude, which is easily calculated as the square root of the sum of the squares of the two wind vectors (u and v).
